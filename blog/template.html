The core challenges of real-time remote sensor access are no secret: reliable power and Internet connectivity. In this article, I argue that these challenges are now solvable using recent developments in solar power, battery technology, and satellite connectivity.

Off-the-grid power-intensive applications are a challenge

Although battery technology has improved tremendously in the past decade, especially with the commodification of lithium-ion battery cells now found everywhere from cars to phones, building a self contained battery grid in remote areas still requires significant effort given lithium-ion battery sensitivity to extreme temperatures. Without protection from heat and humidity, lithium-ion cells rapidly degrade and would could fail in the field.

Most off-the-grid communications systems today are powered by home-grown solar grids, often using off the shelf gel cell or lead acid batteries. Home-grown solar grids and battery arrays are not only expensive to install, but often require regular maintainance and additional expense to keep functional. For truly remote field sensors, regular maintainance simply isn't tolerable.

Satellite connectivity is finally decent

Until a few years ago, satellite internet was limited Kbps speeds with monthly service costs often exceeding $1,000 per month for limited data packages. The latest generation of Ka band geostationary satellites including ViaSat-2 (launched in June, 2017), and HughesNet EchoStar XIX (launched in December 2016) offer 300 Gbit/s throughput and enable ground terminal uplinks around 3-4 Mbps for a service cost under $200 per month. This latest class of satellite technology can finally support the high-bandwidth sensor applications required by industry including real-time video serveillance (2.5 Mbps for HD 720p) and continuous sensor data streaming (typically 1 Mbps) of many existing SCADA systems.

With new satellite internet providers including SpaceX, OneWeb, and Boeing beginning to offer low earth orbit services, satellite internet service will only improve in the coming decades. Limitations of current providers including high latency and poor polar coverage will be solved and will eventually bring satellite internet into applications both remote and urban as a competitive alternative to traditional cable services.

The super secret masterplan

The super secret masterplan, then, is rather logical. By taking advantage of recent developments in solar, battery, and broadband satellite technology, I intend to develop a discrete terminal unit known as the Scout AI Core which can exist fully independent of existing power and connectivity infrastructure indefinitely by relying on renewable solar power and satellite internet. With the Scout AI Core, I intend to offer a service that has so far remained out of reach (except for the odd home-grown solution) even for the many multi-billion dollar industries that operate in the remote world every day: real-time access to what's happening on the ground where it matters most.

As the consumer satellite internet industry develops in the coming decade and makes satellite internet accessible to more and more of the world's population, small-scale power grids for remote sensors will still be required for installations outside of urban centers. The Scout AI Core will not be made irrelevant by technology advancement, but will become more capable because of it in line with the advantages of the next geration of low Earth orbit satellites.

The Scout AI Core has been designed to easily allow real-time access to the most remote corners of the world. So long as there are still remote corners of the world, Scout will still have a purpose.
