A new generation of connected hardware

  • Zane Mountcastle
    October 31, 2019

As the web becomes oversaturated with increasingly niche product offerings, the physical world remains remarkably devoid of intelligent hardware. The ‘IoT revolution’ has so far been largely inconsequential not because of a lack of need or even because of a lack of physical hardware innovation. Instead, its central weakness is an outdated business model based on slim hardware margins rather than the capitalizing on ever-improving software-defined features to flip the depreciation curve.

The old model

Yet, as Moore’s Law slowly flattens under the unfortunate constraints of the universal laws of physics, consumers are no longer driven to regularly replace hardware for the sake of new hardware. Consumers are holding onto devices longer and longer since there is little incentive to upgrade and software is continually released for free. Anecdotally speaking, the laptop I’m writing this post on was assembled in 2013 and I see no need to replace it in the near term.

The old business model of electronics hardware is outdated and has failed to keep pace with the shifting dynamics of consumer demand for devices that continue to provide value years after the hardware itself is initially purchased. Consumers demand continually improved software and producers rightfully oblige (software is scalable, so why not?), yet the pre-Internet era business models of most consumer electronics companies rely on one-time hardware revenue with little planning for benefitting from the device’s continued value in the event of consumer resistance purchasing the latest and greatest hardware model.

The new model

As the Internet pervades every corner of our planet, the software powering physical devices become the core source of continued (and often appreciating) value to end-users. Bugs fixes can be pushed to remote devices; new features can be developed and unlocked on existing hardware. Generalized hardware is now developed years ahead of its time to accommodate the slow, steady stream of updates and features that would, in times past, have remained at the factory for the next hardware model.

Software as the core value-add of hardware is not new. Apple and Microsoft have been pushing OS updates to devices for years. Lately, over-the-air (OTA) software updates have made the leap from computers and smartphones to cars, exercise equipment, and remote industrial sensors.

However, only very recently have consumer electronics hardware companies seen recurring revenue from software-based services through existing physical hardware. Peloton has pioneered a new business model with an Internet-connected stationary bike empowered by a monthly $39 subscription for live classes; subscriptions now account for nearly 30% of revenue at zero marginal cost. SimpliSafe offers inexpensive home security hardware alongside a home security monitoring subscription to glean practical value from the devices otherwise streaming raw, minimally valuable raw data. Picogrid offers remote camera and sensor terminals with a monthly subscription for automated monitoring of petabytes of raw data.

As electronics hardware becomes increasingly commoditized, it is becoming little more than a delivery mechanism for software-produced value. Consumer electronics hardware stands to reach the legendary profitability of software in the coming years not by selling more product as the old model would dictate, but by adopting a business model based on software itself.

With cheap, commoditized hardware empowered by intelligent, continuously improved software services, businesses will finally directly benefit from their continued software development efforts and do so reliably for years at a time.
